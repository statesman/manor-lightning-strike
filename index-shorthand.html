After the storm

Lightning strike, flooding galvanize Manor family’s faith

Story by Patrick Beach. Photos, video and audio by Reshma Kirpalani.
Edited by Roberto Villalpando. Interactive presentation by Eric Webb.

Doing major repairs or renovations on a home in our part of the world in the summer is often hard, hot, nasty work.

Now imagine doing that work during a month when your religion instructs you to fast from dawn until sunset — no eating, no drinking.

That’s the situation Mohammed Abdul Majeed and his wife, Tasneem, find themselves in. In early May, the home they live in with their two children and daughter-in-law in the Carriage Hills subdivision in the eastern end of Manor was struck by lightning during a storm. There was a fire, and they could see sky where part of the roof should have been.

Since then, the rains haven’t stopped. Tarps on the roof flap in the breeze and provide less-than-perfect shelter from the weather, but the inside of the home is a skeleton. The walls, the furniture — everything has been soaked.

And now it’s Ramadan, the month in which observant Muslims fast every day from dawn until sunset as one of the Five Pillars of Islam, or the five foundational acts of faith.

On July 4, a construction company started rebuilding the Majeed family home, which was struck by lightning on May 5 and destroyed by the fire and floods that followed. The family, which is not insured, raised more than $47,000 for repairs by the beginning of July through a GoFundMe account. Repairs for the roof alone are an estimated $18,000.

Mohammed Abdul Majeed said that his family, which paid off the house five years ago, had achieved the "American Dream." Despite the extensive repairs required to fix the house and the lack of insurance money to do so, he fully believes that his family can achieve this dream again.

“It was beautiful,” Tasneem said of her house last week, looking at beams, a few salvaged items and a “For Sale” sign the family had in their yard for a time without success. Sodden furniture sat in the driveway, free for the taking. And Tasneem said there’s mold in what few walls remain, including the one between the garage and the inside of the house.

The misery the spring and early summer rains inflicted on thousands in Central Texas came early and hard to the Majeeds. But they say it’s because of God’s mercy that no one was hurt. And with God’s will they will rebuild.

“God saved us and took part of the house just to show others, to show the community,” Mohammed said. “Allah tests us. This is the way he tests us. God doesn’t give you a burden you can’t carry.”

They’ve got a place to stay not far away. They have friends in the Muslim community and the community at large. The house is paid for, but it wasn’t insured. As some but not all Muslims do, Mohammed believes a homeowners policy is essentially gambling with no guarantee of benefit.

Above: Watch a video that the Majeed family uploaded to YouTube, which shows their cleanup efforts during Tropical Storm Bill. 

“Many Muslims think I should take insurance,” Mohammed said. “My study says all this is biting the poor and making the rich richer. That is not a balanced way of life. We would rather pay the poor, the travelers, the beggars. The rich get richer. They keep the money and accrue interest, which we don’t accept.” Many Muslims also heed the Quran’s prohibition on paying or receiving interest.

In observance of religious and cultural tradition, the family used to take their shoes off before entering the home. That ban is no longer in effect. The place where they prayed five times a day is a work zone.

And work they do. Mohammed figures it’ll take another six months. The couple’s son, meanwhile, has started a GoFundMe drive that’s raised close to $50,000. They’re hoping to make it to $100,000. Not a lot of money, just enough to get back to where they were.

Mohammed isn’t working just yet. He used to work for Home Depot and asked for a leave of absence after the lightning strike, fire and floods. He says he was refused.

So here they stand, seeing what many would see as a curse as a blessing instead.

“We walk all our lives to our destiny,” Mohammed said. “This is the proof.”

Farah Majeed was a senior in high school when her family's home was struck by lightning. Although she graduated, she missed many of the celebratory end-of-year events due to extensive repairs that were required for the home, some of which the family completed own its own.
